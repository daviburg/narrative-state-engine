The longhouse hums with life again, yet the world feels subtly larger than when I left it. After recounting the journey, I allow silence to settle before speaking again--not as an explorer returning, but as one guiding the next season. The Land and the Harvest "The mountain changes nothing if we fail to change with it," I say quietly. I walk the cultivated plots with Borin and Lena, examining what survived the growing season. We mark which plants endured the cold longest and set aside seeds from the strongest growth only. I ask Borin to expand drying racks and prepare a second, smaller kiln nearer the fields so food preservation does not depend on a single structure. Before the ground hardens fully, I organize work parties to: deepen soil beds with ash and refuse from the longhouse hearth, build low windbreak walls of stone and packed snow, and experiment with a covered growing patch using hide and bone frames to test whether plants can survive deeper into winter. We are no longer merely harvesting--we are learning how the land wishes to feed us. Adjusting the Hunts That evening I sit with Kael and the hunters. "The herds thin because too many eyes now follow them," I say. We change our pattern: smaller hunting parties, longer rotations, preservation of breeding animals where possible. Two hunters begin mapping migration paths rather than pursuing kills, learning scarcity before it becomes hunger. The Southern Tribes I ask Kael to select two calm-tempered hunters, not warriors, to prepare gifts: dried meat, fermented vegetables, and carved tools. "When the southern tribes circle close," I tell him, "they must meet neighbors, not rivals." A meeting place is chosen beyond our traps where neither side claims dominance. We will invite conversation before suspicion grows. The Trapper's Story I ask for the hunter who met the lone trapper. Together we retrace his description upon the snow using sticks and stones, mapping rivers and distances as best memory allows. "If glowing stones travel rivers," I reason aloud, "then rivers carry news faster than people." I prepare a small trade bundle and instruct that, if the trapper returns or is found again, he is welcomed openly and offered warmth, stories, and safe passage. A friend who walks between worlds is worth more than many hunters. Study of the Fragment Near the longhouse entrance, I establish a dedicated place for observation. The fragment rests upon stone, never touched directly. Each evening we record: its pulse, temperature changes, dreams reported nearby, animal reactions. Anya is allowed to observe but never alone. Knowledge must grow slower than danger. Anya At dusk I walk with her beyond the noise of camp. "You listened while I was gone," I tell her. I teach her a new exercise: not sensing power, but rhythm. Together we sit facing the mountains, counting breaths between distant pulses, learning patience rather than reach. If her dreams return, she will describe them through drawings rather than words. Meaning often arrives before language. The Family That night, when duties finally release us, I sit beside Kael in the quiet warmth of the longhouse, the sounds of the tribe softened by distance. "The world grows," I tell him gently, resting my hand in his. "So must we." For the first time since returning, I allow myself to set aside leadership and uncertainty, choosing closeness and continuity--trusting that life itself is also a form of preparation for what comes next. Autumn deepens around us, and I watch carefully to see which of these paths the changing world answers first.
