# turn-204 — PLAYER

The mountain has answered once. That is enough for tonight. I do not continue testing. Instead, I prepare an observation. The fragment I carry is wrapped in layered cloth and placed at the center of our camp, equidistant from each of us. Around it, I mark a small circle in the frost and score reference lines pointing toward the mountains. Before resting, I compare its faint pulse to the distant rhythm of the peaks, committing the timing to memory. Then I speak quietly to Rurik and Boruk: "We learn nothing more by pressing tonight. Now we learn whether the mountain changes the world when unwatched." We extinguish the fire early and keep alternating watch through the night, observing without interference: whether the fragment's pulse strengthens or weakens, whether frost forms differently near it, whether sound, wind, or dreams shift while we sleep. At dawn, before moving camp, I immediately return to the boundary markings we carved the previous night and compare them to the present edge. If the sterile line has advanced, receded, or altered shape, I measure and record the change. Only after this comparison do we decide whether to continue forward or withdraw with what we have learned.
