# turn-159 — DM

Having laid the groundwork for mathematics through song and dance, you shift your focus to visual understanding. After the counting lessons, you lead your small band of pupils outside the longhouse, or to a cleared patch of earth within it when the weather is too harsh. With a stick in hand, you begin to draw in the snow, or etch lines in the packed dirt. Simple shapes emerge: a straight line representing a path, a circle for the moon, a square for the longhouse. You weave these shapes into stories, animating stick figures to represent hunters, and using the round moon and sun as characters in tales of the changing seasons. The children, captivated, begin to mimic your actions, their small fingers tracing tentative lines, creating their own interpretations. They learn that a triangle can be a mountain, a series of parallel lines the tracks of a deer. Slowly, imperceptibly, the abstract concepts of geometry and rudimentary symbols for writing begin to take root in their young minds, paving the way for more complex understanding. The shaman, passing by, observes your lessons with a quiet, approving nod, a rare, almost imperceptible smile playing on his lips.
