# turn-217 — DM

Winter had truly settled, a profound blanket of white and an unyielding cold that transformed the world. Within the longhouse, the council gathered, faces serious but resolute, ready to measure what had endured. You walked the three growing sites in silence, the frigid air biting. The outermost patch, despite its reinforced design, had yielded completely, its frail life snuffed out by the relentless cold. The second, more robustly covered patch, showed a few withered remnants, a stubborn defiance that ultimately failed. But the sheltered plot closest to the longhouse, warmed by its residual heat and enriched soil, held a small, incredible victory: a few stalks of arctic dock, though stunted and bruised, still stubbornly green at their core. "This," you declared, a quiet triumph in your voice, "endures. We commit to this." You tasked Borin with refining this method, to expand the sheltered plots as soon as the spring hinted at return. Kael's report on the southern tribes was nuanced. Their delegation had honored the shared boundaries for hunting, a fragile trust beginning to form. They had not sent envoys, but word had reached you through a returning hunter: the southern tribes themselves were under increasing pressure from even further south, explaining their northward push. The knowledge of preservation techniques had been accepted with surprising gratitude, a testament to your proactive diplomacy. The rivers, now locked in solid ice, offered little new. Scouts confirmed no strange stones in the frozen flow, no new outsiders tracking north. The faint rumor of sickness remained distant, though a few hunters noted an increased number of small animal carcasses in certain forested areas, their deaths seemingly unrelated to predator activity or hunger. A full cycle of observation of the fragment had indeed passed. Reviewing the records, a pattern emerged: the fragment's pulse was largely independent of the coldest nights or the brightest stars, maintaining its own slow, steady, alien rhythm. It occasionally seemed to *resonate* with moments of extreme cold, or when a specific constellation, the 'Silent Hunter' your people called it, reached its zenith, but these were correlations, not causes. Its cycle was internal, a heartbeat alien to the natural world. Anya, when asked to demonstrate the frost line once more, performed with newfound precision. The delicate line of rime formed almost instantly, sharp and unwavering. You extended the test: a longer line, a subtle curve, and then a repetition. She succeeded, her concentration absolute, her control growing. You tasked her with tracing a simple, recognizable symbol in frost, to see if her growing control could impart intent. Kael's report on the tribe was stark but hopeful. Hunting returns had stabilized under the new, stricter patterns, a testament to discipline, but the sheer abundance of game was lessened. No sickness had touched your people, a profound blessing you attributed to the improved diet and vigilant sanitation. Morale, despite the relentless cold and the known external pressures, remained high, a quiet strength forged in shared purpose and leadership. Lyrawyn, when asked what changes when winter deepens, did not guess. "The snow grows deep, the wind bites the skin, and the animals sleep for a long time," she stated, her small voice clear, her observations precise. Faelan, though barely more than an infant, showed remarkable resilience to the cold, sleeping soundly through the harshest nights, his cries strong but quickly soothed, a sturdy little anchor in the heart of the longhouse. That night, in the quiet warmth of the longhouse, you sat beside Kael, listening to the soft murmur of the sleeping tribe. Winter had settled, and enough time had passed. You listened to your body, to the subtle changes within. A familiar lightness, a heightened sense of smell, a deeper, almost electric hum beneath your skin--the unmistakable signs were there. Life had indeed taken root again. The tribe would carry not only through this winter, but beyond it, growing. The cold had spoken, and you had listened for what had changed, and for what had profoundly begun.
