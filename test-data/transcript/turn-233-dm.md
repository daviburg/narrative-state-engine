# turn-233 — DM

"It travels with the living," you state quietly, your gaze sweeping over Kael and the healer, a stark acknowledgment of the unseen threat. "That is all we needed to know first." You rise, turning fully to face the tribe, your voice carrying clearly over the muted sounds of the awakening wilderness. "No one approaches without purpose. No one touches him. No one who comes near him returns to the longhouse without passing through fire, cold, and time." Kael, understanding the gravity of your words, immediately begins to establish a hard boundary, marking a perimeter that no one is to cross without your express permission. The designated healer remains by the man, a silent sentinel, placing small portions of food within his reach, but never making contact. A single watcher, vigilant and focused, is assigned to observe from a safe distance, to be rotated periodically, never lingering long enough to risk exposure. The children and elders are kept far from this side of the settlement, their safety paramount. You turn back to the man, his weakened state a testament to the illness he carries. "You ran from it," you acknowledge, your voice devoid of judgment. "That was wisdom. Now you will help us understand it." You instruct the healer to offer small, easily digestible amounts of food, observing not just his consumption, but his reaction, gauging his strength, his ability to hold it down. This is not about rapid recovery, but methodical observation. The study begins, structured and precise. You watch the frequency and intensity of his coughs, the subtle changes in the angry red marks on his skin, noting whether warmth improves or worsens his condition, and how the water he consumed seems to affect him. Between his ragged breaths, you begin your probing questions. "When did it begin? How long before others fell? What came first - the fever, the cough, or the marks? Did any survive longer than the rest? Did anyone recover?" He answers haltingly, his voice a dry rasp, each word an effort. The fever, always the fever first, then the cough, then the marks, red blossoms of disease across the skin. Some fell within days, others lingered a week or more. No, he knew of no one who recovered. He is a testament to the illness, a living, breathing account of its merciless progression. You kneel once more, placing your hand on the cold, damp earth. You reach outward again, quietly, to the small, unseen denizens of the thawing ground: the burrowing beetles, the silent worms. You ask: "Do those who fall remain as they are? Or does something change after death?" The whispers from below are chilling. The bodies, they confirm, do not remain. Something consumes them, something that moves with a terrible hunger beneath the earth, leaving only bones. The sickness, it seems, has a hungry aftermath. Finally, your gaze drifts to the fragment, still resting at the longhouse. "If this shapes what it touches," you muse quietly to yourself, "then we do not bring it here. We decide - later - if we bring anything to it." You step back, creating distance once more between yourself, the sick man, and the rest of the tribe. "This is not an enemy," you tell Kael, "But it is not yet something we welcome. We learn it first. Then we decide whether we fight it - or prevent it."
