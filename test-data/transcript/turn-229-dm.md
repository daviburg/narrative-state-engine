# turn-229 — DM

"Nothing reaches us without being seen twice," you declare, your voice firm but quiet, as you turn to face the path the stranger has taken. You reach outward, your senses extending beyond the immediate, seeking the quiet language of the wild. To the circling ravens, the scuttling voles, and the nervous rabbit frozen by the approaching man, you ask: "What passed here before this one? Was he alone? Was he followed? Did others fall where he walks?" The creatures respond in a cacophony of instincts and fragmented images. The ravens, circling high above, caw of a lone figure, persistent but slow, leaving a trail of uneven prints. They speak of no others, no pursuers, only the vast emptiness of the tundra behind him. The voles, burrowing beneath the melting snow, felt only the vibration of one heavy tread, no accompanying lighter steps. The rabbit, quivering in its tracks, had witnessed only the solitary passage, its fear an echo of his own solitude. The land itself, through its creatures, confirms he is alone, and has been for some time. You turn to Kael, your gaze unwavering. "No one touches him yet. No one brings him inside." Your words are measured, calm. The preparations are swift and silent: a temporary shelter of hides and furs is erected downwind from the longhouse, a safe distance to protect the tribe, yet close enough for observation. Water is placed within, along with a small portion of dried meat, an offering that acknowledges his humanity without compromising the tribe's safety. With the tribe's security ensured, you allow your own form to shift. Not into something grotesque or false, but into a subtle alteration: your features soften, the sharp lines of your elven heritage becoming less pronounced, your height seeming to diminish slightly, your luminous skin taking on a more earthen tone. You become, for this encounter, someone less recognizable, a tribal healer perhaps, rather than the undisputed leader. With a trusted healer and a watchful hunter at your side, you begin your approach. As you draw near, the man's condition is starkly apparent. His breath is shallow, rasping in his chest, and his skin, visible beneath torn furs, is unnaturally pale, dotted with angry red splotches. His eyes are sunken, but a flicker of desperate hope ignites within them as he spots you. He clutches a small, leather-bound satchel, the only possession he seems to have, but bears no obvious weapons. There are no distinguishing markings on his tattered clothing, only the grime and wear of a long, arduous journey. "You have reached shelter," you say, your voice calm and measured, carrying across the still, damp air of the early spring. Your eyes bore into his, bypassing the physical, seeking the truth within. "Now tell me," you challenge, "what has been following you that you could not outrun?"
