The pulse continues, an unyielding rhythm, and you exhale slowly. "Not silence," you murmur to Rurik and Boruk, the wind snatching your words. "Incomprehension." With care, you erase the hexagonal carving, leaving no trace of your failed attempt at dialogue, and secure the fragment once more. "We do not force understanding," you declare quietly, "We prepare for it." You cast one last, long look at the mountains, imprinting their shape, their eerie light, and the boundary's subtle retreat into your memory. At first light, you turn homeward. The journey back is a quiet march of purpose. You conserve your strength, your thoughts already racing ahead to the tasks that await. The fragment you carry is a silent instructor, its faint hum a constant reminder of the alien presence. You plan: a dedicated place for its study, instruments to chart the mountain's rhythm, and, most importantly, minds, like Anya's, trained to perceive patterns beyond instinct alone. The mountain, you realize, is not an enemy to be fought, but a question to be answered--a question you are not yet equipped to ask correctly. The days blend into a routine of travel. Snowfields are crossed, frozen streams navigated, and watches kept without incident. You do not hunt beyond necessity, your mission one of observation, not conquest. By the sixth evening, a thin plume of smoke, a living thread against the vast arctic sky, rises from the horizon--the longhouse. A quiet relief settles in your chest. As you draw closer, the wolf-dog senses your approach first, its bark carrying across the snow. Figures emerge from the settlement: hunters, apprentices, children, and elders gathering instinctively. Kael stands at the front, his arms folded, his face etched with concern that gives way to undeniable relief as he sees you. You do not pause outside the circle of life you have built. You walk directly into it, towards Kael, towards your family, towards your people. "The mountains breathe," you say simply, your voice carrying over the murmurs of the crowd, "And they listen--though not yet in ways we understand." You then place the wrapped fragment carefully upon a prepared stone near the longhouse entrance, its cold presence openly presented to the tribe. "Call Lena, the Shaman, and Anya," you add, "There is much to share." As the tribe gathers closer, you begin recounting the journey--the retreating boundary, the strange geometric marks, the pulse that answered but did not threaten. You speak plainly, weaving the complex into narratives that hunters and children alike can grasp, watching their faces. Some step forward without fear, their eyes alight with curiosity. Others recoil slightly, a flicker of unease in their gaze. You note Anya, her eyes fixed on the fragment, a subtle tension in her small frame, as if feeling something she cannot yet name.
