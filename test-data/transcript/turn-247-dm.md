# turn-247 — DM

"If it bought him time once, then haste is the one thing that can steal it back," you declare, holding off the second attempt. You initiate a change in the field, not around the man, but around the intricate dance between you and the sickness. "Prepare the Broken Circle," you instruct the Shaman. He moves with ancient grace, a silent counterpoint to the arctic wind. With ash gathered from the longhouse hearth and a handful of dried, pungent herbs from his pouch, he traces a precise circle on the thawing earth around your working space. It is not a barrier to the man, but a ward against invasive patterns, a symbol of unmaking supporting your intent. You turn to Anya, her concentration unwavering. "Not stronger. Cleaner. You will make only a single shorter cut, across the next visible path. Then stop. No reaching. No holding. No second motion." Her brow furrows in fierce focus, her control a sharpened blade. "Mark the time before the cut," you tell the healer. "Mark the breath before the cut. Mark the color of the lesions before the cut." The healer nods, already poised, stylus and slate ready. The man, instructed to observe only three things--heat, pain, and buzzing--lies still, his eyes wide with a desperate, understanding gaze. You watch the pattern within him again with arcane sight. The intricate, glowing lines of the sickness pulse with their unsettling rhythm, slowly advancing. You wait, patient, until a distinct internal pulse crests along a new path, a fresh line of advance appearing across his chest. "Now," you nod to Anya. A single, shimmering arc of frost, impossibly fine, flashes into existence, bisecting the fresh path of the illness. And then, stillness. No one speaks for a full interval, save for the healer's soft murmurs of observation. His breath, for a measurable span, becomes shallower, more regular, the ragged edge momentarily smoothed. The green pulse in the lesions along the affected path dims visibly, a brief, almost ghostly flicker. And the man, his eyes now closed, whispers, "The buzzing... it stopped. Just for a moment." The heat, he confirms, lessened, and the pain receded, if only slightly. The hesitation has deepened. The lesions dimmed longer. His breathing gained another fraction of ground. You look to Kael, your voice quiet but firm. "If this works, even a little, no rumor leaves this boundary until we understand it." Then, to the Shaman, you reiterate, "We are not healing him yet. We are teaching the sickness how to fail." This is not a battle of brute force, but of unraveling.
