I raise one hand for stillness. "No second attempt yet." We do not chase the first reaction. We let it settle. --- I watch the man closely through the next interval. Not for drama. For consequence. I want to know: - whether the fever's rhythm returns exactly as before, - whether the green pulse in the lesions resumes its old pattern or remains slightly out of step, - whether his breathing steadies, worsens, or gains even a little ground. If the disruption bought him even a small measure of time, that matters. --- I turn to Anya. "You did not strike it," I tell her quietly. "You caught its rhythm and made it stumble." "That is enough." I do not let her try again immediately. I make her rest, breathe, and repeat the pattern only in the air with her finger, with no frost, until her hand and will are calm again. Precision must outlast excitement. --- To the Shaman, I say: "We do not break it all at once. We interrupt it where it thinks itself perfect." I ask him whether he knows any old signs, chants, or warding forms used not to banish sickness, but to break curses, compulsions, or invasive influences that move in lines and repetitions. Not to replace what worked. To support it. --- Then I return my full attention to the man. "Tell me what changed," I say. "Did the heat lessen, even for a breath?" "Did the pain move?" "Did anything inside you stop--or shift?" I want his lived sense of the pattern, not only what I can see. --- If, by the end of the interval, there is any sign at all that the pattern did not fully recover-- even slight-- then I prepare a second attempt. Not the same crossing line. A shorter interruption, placed across the next visible path of spread. One cut, then stop. Watch again. No haste. No force. We win by making it lose its shape. --- If instead the pattern returns completely unchanged, or the man worsens from the interruption, then we do not repeat at once. We change strategy. We study timing, not strength. The next attempt would come only at the exact moment the internal pulse peaks. --- I look to Kael without taking my eyes off the patient. "Hold the boundary." Then to the healer: "Mark every change." Then, quietly, to myself: "It can stumble. Now we learn whether it can fail."
