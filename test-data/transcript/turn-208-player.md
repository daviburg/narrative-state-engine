The pulse continues, unchanged, and I exhale slowly. "Not silence," I murmur to Rurik and Boruk. "Incomprehension." I erase the hexagon carefully, disturbing nothing else. The fragment is wrapped again and secured. "We do not force understanding," I say quietly. "We prepare for it." Before leaving, I study the mountains one last time, fixing their rhythm, their light, and the boundary's behavior firmly in memory. At first light, we turn back toward the longhouse. We travel with urgency but not fear, carrying knowledge rather than victory. On the journey home, I begin planning what must come next: a place to study the fragment safely, instruments to measure the mountain's rhythm, and minds trained to perceive patterns beyond instinct. The mountain is not an enemy. It is a question we are not yet able to ask correctly. We travel with steady purpose, avoiding unnecessary detours. The sterile boundary fades gradually behind us, replaced by the familiar rhythm of wind, animal tracks, and living cold. Each evening I compare the fragment's faint pulse to memory rather than testing it again, conserving strength and thought for what must follow. The journey becomes routine: snowfields crossed, frozen streams navigated, watch rotations kept without incident. We do not hunt beyond necessity. We return as observers, not conquerors. By the sixth evening, smoke from the longhouse rises against the horizon -- a thin, living thread against the arctic sky. Relief settles quietly in my chest. As we approach, the wolf-dog senses us first, its bark carrying across the snow. Figures emerge from the settlement: hunters, apprentices, children, and elders gathering instinctively. Kael stands at the front, arms folded, watchful but unmistakably relieved. I do not pause outside the circle of life we built. I walk directly into it. "The mountains breathe," I say simply to those gathered. "And they listen -- though not yet in ways we understand." I place the wrapped fragment carefully upon a prepared stone near the longhouse entrance rather than keeping it hidden, allowing the tribe to witness it openly. "Call Lena, the Shaman, and Anya," I add. "There is much to share." As people gather closer, I begin recounting the journey -- the retreating boundary, the geometric marks, the pulse that answered but did not threaten -- speaking plainly so hunters and children alike can understand. While I speak, I watch the crowd carefully: who steps forward without fear, who recoils, who feels something they cannot name. And only then do I truly look around the camp, noticing who else may have arrived during my absence -- travelers, strangers, or signs that the wider world has begun to move while we were away.
