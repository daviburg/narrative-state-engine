# turn-294 — DM

"These are not only directions," you state, your voice carrying through the longhouse, "They are things you will see done." You do not linger at the center. If The Quiet Weave is to hold, it must move through others. You walk with Kael, your boots treading the thawing earth. Kneeling where the soil darkens, you press your hand into it, showing how it holds shape, how water drains without vanishing. "Here," you instruct, "not because it grows now, but because it will continue to grow." You then guide him to thinner, higher ground. "This feeds the hunt, not us. Here we do not plant. We leave it, so something else can live." The unspoken lesson hangs in the crisp air: understanding the land's inherent purpose. "You will choose the next place," you tell him, "and I will say nothing--unless you are wrong." With Lena, you observe Borin and the other crafters. "The children will build," you begin, "but they will also learn from those who already shape." You describe the rotation of children through the workshops, observing and assisting, becoming capable. Then, you sketch a simple wedge in the dirt. "A tool that does not strike straight," you explain, "it separates. Make one, test it, then improve it only if it fails." You sit by the fragment with the Shaman and Anya, a silent vigil. "We observe together," you whisper, "not as teacher and student, but as those who do not yet understand." You subtly shift your position, nearer, then farther, guiding Anya to notice, not to reach. "If it reacts, we record. If it does not, we record. This is how we learn something that does not speak." Each evening, you sit with the healer, preparing bark and charcoal. "We do not keep this in memory alone," you explain, "We place it where others can find it." You begin recording plant properties, their strengths, their failures, their seasons - "not stories, but patterns." To Borin, you build a small, layered model of the new structure, demonstrating air gaps, raised floors, and separated storage. "Heat rises. Moisture settles. Air must move--but not freely. You will not copy this. You will understand it. Then you build your version." Finally, addressing the southern elders, you assign each of their shamans to a leader--Kael, Lena, the Shaman, the healer. "You do not interfere. You watch. You remember." Your gaze is firm. "You will return to your people. And when you come back, you bring one thing. Not many. Not stories. One piece of knowledge your people hold that we do not. If we are to share what we know, we will not be the only ones who give." You step back, allowing the knowledge to take root, moving silently between the groups, watching, correcting only when needed, trusting the nascent structure of The Quiet Weave to grow.
