I do not move closer. The answer is enough. "It travels with the living," I say quietly, more to Kael and the healer than to the man. "That is all we needed to know first." --- I rise and turn fully to the tribe, my voice carrying clearly now. "No one approaches without purpose." "No one touches him." "No one who comes near him returns to the longhouse without passing through fire, cold, and time." I assign roles immediately: - Kael establishes a hard boundary--no one crosses it without my word. - The healer remains, but does not touch--only observes and places what is needed. - One watcher only, rotated, never lingering beyond what is required. "The children and elders remain far from this side of the settlement." "This does not reach them." --- I turn back to the man. "You ran from it," I say. "That was wisdom." "Now you will help us understand it." --- I gesture to the healer again. "Food. Small amounts. Left within reach." "We do not strengthen him quickly--we observe what he can hold." --- Then I begin the study. Not rushed. Not emotional. Structured. I watch: - how often he coughs, - whether the red marks change with time or effort, - whether warmth improves or worsens his state, - whether water restores or weakens him. I ask him, slowly, between breaths: "When did it begin?" "How long before others fell?" "What came first--the fever, the cough, or the marks?" "Did any survive longer than the rest?" "Did anyone recover?" --- As he answers, I listen not only to him--but to the pattern. --- Then I turn once more to the land, quietly. "To those beneath and around--" "Do those who fall remain as they are?" "Or does something change after death?" --- Finally, I look to the fragment, still resting at the longhouse. Not to use it. But to consider it. "If this shapes what it touches," I say quietly to myself, "then we do not bring it here." "We decide--later--if we bring anything to it." --- I step back, creating distance again. "This is not an enemy," I say to Kael. "But it is not yet something we welcome." "We learn it first." "Then we decide whether we fight it--or prevent it."
